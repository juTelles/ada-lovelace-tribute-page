Ada Lovelace

Countess of Lovelace, the first programmer

The life and work of the Countess of Lovelace

  • 1815 - London, England, on december tenth of 1815, Augusta Ada Byron was born, daughter of George Gordon Byron, 6th, or simply Lord Byron, an English Poet with an unbecoming reputation and a bad temper, and Anne Isabella Noel Byron, Lady Byron, an English mathematician, who was also a very religious and rigid woman.
  • 1816 - In january of 1816, the couple’s finances had become a matter of concern and Lord Byron’s bad temper had only got worse. As a temporary measure Lord Byron requested that Lady Byron and Ada went to live with Lady Byron’s parents until he could put their finances in order. What was supposed to be temporary became permanent, Lady Baron believed her husband was mad and found out facts about him that her strong moral beliefs could not tolerate. In that same year, by legal advice and with her parents' incentive she sent Lord Biron a letter asking for legal separation, at the beginning he refused to accept, but at the threat of having some of his immoral acts becoming public he at last accepted.
  • 1824 - Lord Baron died, letting Lady Baron as the sole custodian of Ada, when she was only eight years old. Lady Baron was herself a mathematician, considered to be very intelligent since her childhood, she was given an education to the level of a cambridge scholar. With her own love of exact sciences and the idea that living and breathing a rigid education could prevent her daughter to develop her father's so called madness, Ada's several private tutors were instructed to teach her mathematics and sciences from a very early age.
  • 1833 - At seventeen, Ada had become very talented in mathematics and with the help Mary Somerville, one of her tutors and also a very bright mind, Ada met Charles Babbage, the so called "father of computers", only a month or so later Babbage presented Ada to his machine, the "difference engine" and she was mesmerized by it. 1842 - Now an adult of substantial knowledge, Ada was asked to translate from french to english a transcription, written by the italian mathematician Luigi Menabrea, of an lecture given by Babbage in the university of Turin, in which he participated and transcribed Bannage's ideas about his future new machine: the "Analytical Engine".
  • 1835 - Ada Byron became Ada King, due to her marriege to William, 8th Baron, the couple had 3 children together, in 1836 was born Byron, in 1837 Ada gave birth to her only daughter, Anne Isabelle after her mather and in 1839 Ada gave birth to her youngster, Ralph Gordon.
  • 1839 - Ada was the sole descendent of the Barons of Lovelace, an important title, and for this her husband was made the next Earl of Lovelace and Ada became, like most know her, the Countess of Lovelace.
  • 1843 - Ada's work was published in the September edition of Taylor's Scientific Memoirs. Ada not only finished the job she was asked, she went way further than a simple translation, she wrote her own take on the Analytical Engine. In a paper she called Notes, Ada explained the machine, it's functionality and structure, and even described algorithms and how they could be implemented in the analytical machine, descriptions that can be considered the first program ever written. Furthermore, Ada had a different take on mathematics than most, at her time, she believed in integrating poetry with science and she applied this concept in the analytical engine machine, she wrote in her notes that the machine was capable of calculating more than numbers, that it was possible to represent real life subjects and objects in numeral form and with this manipulate those represented entities.
  • 1852 - After her work with Babbage, Ada never stopped studying and working on other projects, unfortunately with only 36 years old she developed uterine cancer, and in an age where our medicine was still very much rudimentar, after several months of great disability and pain she died, but her great work and ideas survived through the years in scientific papers, in several novels of all kinds and celebrations of her place in the history of programming.
  • 1980 - Ada, a computer language made for the United States Department of Defense, was named after her.
  • 1981 - The Ada Lovelace Award was created by the Association for Women in Computing. 2009 - She won her own day on the second tuesday of October.
  • 2015 - Her illustration won a place in all new British passports.
  • 2017 - Google made a google doodle in her honor.
  • 2018 - The ninth of October became the National Ada Lovelace Day in the whole United States of America.
  • Conclusion - Over all these years there are several more tributes to Ada, colleges named after her, days on her behalf, prizes in her honor, all made to honor her contributions to science. But history also holds some controversies about Ada’s contributions: some say Ada’s programs, presented on her notes, were actually made by Babbage years before and only published by her, others that she her notes made no real advance only described what Babbage had already found, some even say she wasn’t bright at all. But all, even hers most avide critics, can’t refute two very important facts: Ada was the first to publish a program specifically made to be implemented on a machine and that Ada, with her concept of integrating math and poet, or better yet, math and real life, was the first to see the potential of going beyond the numbers, she theorized that the analytical engine could not only calculate numbers but also abstract objects, who could be adapted to be represented by numbers, and there for, manipulate and calculated. Her concept of math and real life is nothing more than today’s modern concept of math itself and her theory of computing entities is an anticipation of the concept of modern computing or general-purpose computation. Who knows? But, if you ask for my opinion, I would certainly classify such a woman, with a mind years ahead of her time, a brilliant genius, and completely deserving of the title of first computer programming.
